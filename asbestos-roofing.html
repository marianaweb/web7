Asbestos Roofing: What You Need to Know

  • Ahsan Habib
  • May 30, 2024
  • 0 Comments

What is Asbestos?

Asbestos is a group of six naturally occurring fibrous minerals: chrysotile, amosite, crocidolite, anthophyllite, tremolite, and actinolite. Because of its ability to resist heat, fire, electricity, and sound, asbestos was widely used in roofing and siding materials, especially before 1980. While asbestos is not banned by the EPA, it is regulated under the Clean Air Act of 1970. You'll find asbestos in various roofing materials, including shingles, tar, asphalt liquids, roofing felt, caulking, sealant, cement-based shingles, roofing cement, flashings, vents, and corrugated roofing.

What are the Dangers of Asbestos

As long as asbestos is contained, it is generally safe. However, when roofing materials containing asbestos are damaged or disturbed, tiny fibers can become airborne, making the asbestos “friable.” When inhaled or ingested, these fibers can lodge in the respiratory system and lead to serious health issues like mesothelioma, chronic respiratory illness, and asbestosis. Symptoms from asbestos exposure can take 10 to 50 years to appear, and according to the EPA, there is no safe level of asbestos exposure..

Can Asbestos Roofing Be Repaired?

No, asbestos shingles are no longer manufactured, and repairing roofs with asbestos is prohibited by the City of Omaha building code. Additionally, finding matching shingles is nearly impossible since they must be sealed and disposed of as hazardous waste once removed.

How is Asbestos Roofing Removed?

If you suspect your roof contains asbestos, do not inspect or remove it yourself. Hire a professional asbestos contractor. In Nebraska, contractors must notify the state about the job details and obtain approval before proceeding. During removal, contractors wear full protective gear, including respirators, and follow strict procedures to prevent asbestos exposure. They wet the asbestos materials to minimize dust and carefully remove the roofing using a flat pry bar, starting from the top. All materials are sealed in plastic bags and disposed of at designated landfills that accept asbestos waste.

Should You Buy a Home with Asbestos Roofing?

Buying a home with asbestos roofing can be tricky. While intact asbestos roofing is generally safe, replacing it can be costly due to the necessary safety measures. Slate roof tiles containing asbestos can last up to 100 years, but you will likely need to replace the roof eventually. If you suspect asbestos, hire an inspector for a thorough examination and laboratory testing. The findings can influence negotiations and the purchase agreement.

Many insurance companies do not cover asbestos roofs, or may require an inspection and certification of the roof's condition. In areas like Omaha, where hail and strong winds are common, roof replacement is often necessary, making asbestos shingles a potential concern.

In summary, while asbestos roofing can be managed safely if undisturbed, its presence complicates repairs, replacements, and insurance coverage. Professional inspection and careful consideration are crucial when dealing with asbestos in roofing materials.
